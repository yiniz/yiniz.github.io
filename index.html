Yini Zhang

Hi there! Welcome to this tiny corner of mine on the boundless internet!

I’m an assistant professor in the Department of Communication at the University at Buffalo. I graduated with a Ph.D. in mass communication from the School of Journalism & Mass Communication at the University of Wisconsin Madison.

I study the processes and consequences of communication in the hybrid media system. Specifically, I apply computational methods to explore networked actors on social media, information flow across traditional and social media, and their implications for journalism and democracy. My current research program consists of three parts: 1) investigating how people connect and communicate with each other on social media; 2) tracing communication flows across social and news media; and 3) developing computational techniques to facilitate communication research.

I have written academic articles about how social media actors assemble their followings, how social media amplify obscure actors and extremist groups, and how to measure social media public opinion. The topics of those articles range from mass shootings and gun control, immigration, the #MeToo movement, Donald Trump’s social media following, QAnon, to the Russian IRA disinformation campaign. Here’s a link to my Google Scholar.

The methods I use in my research are primarily computational, though I am also conversant with survey and experimental methods. For my research, I usually collect Twitter data via its APIs, conduct automated text analysis using unsupervised and supervised machine learning, perform community detection via various clustering techniques, and run time series modeling. Recently, I have moved beyond Twitter to include other platforms in my research, like Facebook, YouTube, and Reddit. Besides using existing computational methods, I also develop innovative methods in collaboration with colleagues from statistics and computer science to facilitate communication research. R is my primary programming language for research. I have also used SPSS, Hadoop, Java, and Python in the past.

There are a few books that have greatly influenced me during my formative years at SJMC. I’d love to share them with you in case you might be interested: John Zaller’s The Nature and Origins of Mass Opinion, Yochai Benkler’s The Wealth of Networks, Paul Starr’s The Creation of the Media: Political Origins of Modern Communications, Fred Turner’s From Counterculture to Cyberculture: Stewart Brand, the Whole Earth Network, and the Rise of Digital Utopianism, Bruce Bimber, Andrew Flanagin and Cynthia Stohl’s Collective Action in Organizations, David Karpf’s The MoveOn Effect: The Unexpected Transformation of American Political Advocacy, Lance Bennett & Alexandra Segerberg’s The Logic of Connective Action, Zeynep Tufekci’s Twitter and Tear Gas: The Power and Fragility of Networked Protest, Matthew Hindman’s The Myth of Digital Democracy, Alice Marwick’s Status Update: Celebrity, Publicity, and Branding in the Social Media Age, Andrew Chadwick’s The Hybrid Media System: Politics and Power, James Webster’s The Marketplace of Attention, and Nick Couldry’s Media, Society, World: Social Theory and Digital Media Practice. Recently, I was inspired by Yochai Benkler, Robert Faris, and Hal Roberts’s Network Propaganda: Manipulation, Disinformation, and Radicalization in American Politics, Jaime Settle’s Frenemies: How Social Media Polarizes America, and Chris Bail’s Breaking the Social Media Prism: How to Make Our Platforms Less Polarizing. If you happen to be fascinated by any one of these books, I’d be delighted to have a chat with you about it.
