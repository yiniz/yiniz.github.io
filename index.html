Yini Zhang

Hi there! Welcome to this tiny corner of mine on the boundless internet!

I’m an assistant professor in the Department of Communication at the University at Buffalo (some people prefer SUNY-Buffalo). I graduated with a Ph.D. in mass communication from the School of Journalism & Mass Communication at the University of Wisconsin-Madison.

I study the processes and consequences of (mostly political) communication. My research program centers around three questions at three different levels: 1) within social media, how do people express opinions and interact with each other, 2) as an ecosystem, how do social media platforms relate to each other in term of the flow of information, ideas, and debates, and 3) beyond social media, what influence and are influenced by social media activities. With these three lines of inquiry, I hope to explore the nature and dynamics of public attention, opinion expression, and information sharing on social media and their implications for journalism and democracy.

I have written academic articles about how social media actors assemble their followings, how social media amplify obscure actors and extremist groups, and how to measure social media public opinion. The topics of those articles range from mass shootings and gun control, immigration, the #MeToo movement, Donald Trump’s social media following, QAnon, to the Russian IRA disinformation campaign. Here’s a link to my Google Scholar.

The methods I use in my research are primarily computational, though I am also conversant with survey and experimental methods. For my research, I usually collect Twitter data via its APIs, conduct automated text analysis using unsupervised and supervised machine learning, perform community detection via various clustering techniques, and run time series modeling. Recently, I have moved beyond Twitter to include other platforms in my research, like Facebook, YouTube, and Reddit. Besides using existing computational methods, I also develop innovative methods in collaboration with colleagues from statistics and computer science to facilitate communication research. R is my primary programming language. I have a working knowledge of SPSS, Hadoop, Java, and Python.
